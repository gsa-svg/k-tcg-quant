PSA grading is eating One Piece sealed supply: what weekly population data shows

Between June 3 and July 8, 2026, collectors added nearly 100,000 One Piece Card Game cards to PSA's population report — 99,646 new grades across the 21 sets we track, an average of about 16,600 cards per week. Each of those cards came out of a pack, and most of those packs came out of a sealed booster box that no longer exists. This report walks through the weekly grading data we collect set by set, what it says about which sets are being opened right now, and why grading volume is one of the most direct supply signals a sealed-box collector can watch.

Why grading volume matters for sealed boxes

A sealed booster box has two possible futures: it stays sealed, or it gets opened for the cards inside. Every card that shows up in PSA's population report is proof of the second path — someone opened packs, pulled a card worth protecting, and paid a grading fee to slab it. Grading volume is therefore a destruction meter for sealed supply. When weekly grades for a set run hot, boxes of that set are being ripped in quantity; when the pace fades, either the hits have been pulled or opening that set no longer pays. Sustained destruction with steady demand is the classic setup behind long-term sealed appreciation, which is why we chart weekly grades on every set page.

The six-week leaderboard

OP-13 Carrying on His Will dominates the period with 18,303 new grades — more than 18% of all One Piece grading activity — peaking at 3,712 cards in the week of June 17. Its manga-art chase cards, led by Monkey D. Luffy's manga rare, make almost every strong pull worth grading. OP-14 The Azure Sea's Seven follows at 13,749, and OP-12 Legacy of the Master at 7,839. At the other end, OP-16 The Time of Battle registered only 61 grades — it released at the end of May, and the first submissions are only now returning from PSA.

Reading the numbers: three observations

1. Old sets are still being opened

OP-01 Romance Dawn is three and a half years old, its Japanese box has doubled to about $313 this year — and collectors still graded 3,504 of its cards in six weeks. That is destruction working exactly as sealed holders hope: rising box prices have not stopped people from ripping, because the chase cards (and their PSA 10 premiums) rose too. The same is true of OP-05, whose 4,866 grades came while its English box traded above $1,100.

2. The grading wave follows the release wave, with a lag

OP-13 released in Japan in late August 2025 and is still the most-graded set almost a year later, while brand-new OP-16 barely registers. Grading peaks several months after release: it takes time for boxes to be opened, cards to be shipped to PSA, and grades to post. For sealed buyers this lag is useful — a set whose weekly grades are still climbing is a set whose supply is still being destroyed.

3. Gem rates cluster near 87% — and that shapes premiums

Across the 21 sets, gem rates for tracked chase cards run from 83.5% (OP-03) to 90.8% (OP-15), clustering around 87%. Modern One Piece cards come out of the pack clean, so PSA 10s are common — and the graded premium over a raw NM copy stays modest for most cards. Where gem rates dip (older sets with rougher print quality, like OP-03) PSA 10 copies are genuinely scarcer relative to demand, which is one reason early-set graded prices hold up. Methodology and limits

We snapshot PSA's public population report weekly and record the change as that week's new grades. Numbers cover Japanese-language One Piece cards in the sets listed; PSA does not break out language in every case, so mixed-language populations are possible on some entries. Weekly deltas can be slightly lumpy when PSA posts grades in batches. All-time populations count the tracked chase cards per set rather than every card in the set, so they understate total set-wide grading. We publish the series as collected and correct it if PSA restates figures.

FAQ

Does heavy grading make my sealed box more valuable?

Not by itself — it removes sealed supply, which supports prices only if demand holds. OP-15 was heavily graded this period (3,307 cards) while its box price fell 13%, because post-launch supply still outweighed demand. Grading volume is one input, not a verdict.

Why does a new set like OP-16 show almost no grades?

PSA turnaround. Cards from a late-May release only started posting to the population report in late June — its 61 grades will multiply over the coming months as submissions return.

Is a 96% gem rate on OP-16 real?

It is real but early: with only 82 total grades, the rate will move a lot as volume arrives. We flag small samples on set pages for exactly this reason.

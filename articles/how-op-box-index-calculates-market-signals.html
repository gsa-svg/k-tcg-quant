Methodology

How OP Box Index reads One Piece market signals

OP Box Index is built to make sealed One Piece booster box research easier to read. The site does not issue buy or sell calls. It organizes public marketplace signals so collectors can compare sets without jumping between many tabs.

The OPBOX Index, with its actual numbers

The headline number on the dashboard is the OPBOX Index. Its construction is deliberately simple so it cannot be quietly massaged: it is an equal-weight index of 18 Japanese booster boxes that had a tracked price on 2026-01-07, set to 100 on that date. Sets that entered tracking later (OP-02, OP-15, OP-16) are shown individually but excluded from the index so they cannot distort the base. Prices carry forward on days without a fresh observation.

As of 2026-07-23 the index reads 157.4 — Japanese sealed boxes are up 57.4% since January on an equal-weight basis. Because every box gets the same weight, this is not a story about one grail set: it means the typical tracked box, from $67 Royal Blood to $268 Romance Dawn, participated in the move. When the index is flat for a week (as it has been recently at +0%), that tells you the market is digesting rather than trending.

Next to it we publish the Opening Meter: the number of One Piece cards PSA graded in the latest week — currently 10,724 cards/week, up 38.5% versus the prior week. We treat grading volume as a destruction proxy: cards get graded when boxes get opened, so a rising meter means sealed supply is being consumed faster. Reading the two together is the core of the method — index up while the meter runs hot is demand-plus-destruction; index up while the meter cools would be a thinner, more speculative move.

Box price bands

For each sealed box, the site separates low, middle and high price bands when enough market references exist. The middle value is useful as a quick reference, but the spread matters too. A tight spread is usually more reliable than a wide spread with one unusually cheap or expensive listing.

Where we have enough completed sales, the sold median takes priority over any listing band. A concrete example of why: on the day we wrote this, Japanese OP-05's cheapest verified listing ($151.89) sat 28% below its sold median ($211), while Japanese OP-06's cheapest listing ($192.38) sat 67% above its sold median ($115). Listings alone would have ranked the two boxes in the wrong order.

Verified lowest listings

Lowest-price buttons are filtered so loose packs, unrelated products, wrong languages and suspiciously low listings are less likely to appear. When a listing cannot be matched confidently, OP Box Index hides the direct button or falls back to a broader search.

Chase-card concentration

Some sets are carried by one major card. Others have several cards with strong demand. A box can be more resilient when value is spread across multiple chase cards, because demand does not depend on a single pull.

PSA 10 and population context

PSA 10 prices are treated as a separate graded-card market. A high PSA 10 price can signal premium collector demand, but thin sales samples are handled carefully. If the match is uncertain, the price is not shown as a confirmed market value.

Why some prices are hidden

Missing data is intentional when the match quality is weak. Showing no price is better than showing the wrong card, wrong language or wrong product. This is especially important for manga rares, alternate arts and special cards where small title differences can mean a large price difference.

How to use the signals

  • Use sold prices to judge real demand.
  • Use active listings to understand current purchase options.
  • Use chase-card data to understand what supports sealed demand.
  • Use PSA data to judge premium-card strength.
  • Use seller and condition checks before buying anything.

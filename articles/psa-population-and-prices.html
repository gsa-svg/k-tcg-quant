Research Note

How PSA population data affects One Piece card prices

PSA population reports show how many copies of a card have been graded and at what grade. For One Piece chase cards, two numbers matter most: total population (how many copies exist in PSA holders) and gem rate (the share that graded PSA 10).

What population and gem rate mean

  • Total population — the count of all graded copies across every grade, a rough proxy for how many collectors bothered to submit the card.
  • Gem rate — PSA 10 count divided by total population, showing how hard the card is to pull in perfect condition.
  • A card with low total population and a low gem rate is usually the hardest, and most expensive, PSA 10 to find.

Why low population usually means higher prices

  • Fewer graded copies means fewer sellers competing on active listings.
  • A low gem rate signals real print-quality difficulty, not just low submission volume.
  • Chase cards that combine strong character demand with low population tend to set the floor for a set's overall value.

What can change population-driven prices

  • Population grows over time as more collectors submit copies, which can soften scarcity-driven prices.
  • Print-run differences between early and later reprints can shift gem rate for the same card number.
  • A spike in submissions after a card gains attention can temporarily distort short-term population trends.

What the spread actually tells you

The gem rate range across sets is 83.3% (OP-11) to 93.6% (OP-15) — a ten-point spread that changes how you should treat a PSA 10 from each. OP-11 is the hardest set in our table to grade: roughly one in six submissions misses PSA 10. OP-15 and EB-01 sit above 93%, meaning almost every clean copy sent in comes back a 10, so PSA 10 supply for those sets builds quickly and the graded premium stays thin.

Population size tells the other half. OP-05 has been graded 68,854 times — over 129× the volume of OP-16 (532), which is simply too new for submissions to have accumulated. A high count is not automatically bearish; it usually means the set was opened heavily and is widely collected. But when a high count pairs with a high gem rate (OP-04 at 30,600 and 91.4%, OP-07 at 33,648 and 92.7%), graded copies are easy to source and the PSA 10 price behaves much more like the raw price.

This is the mechanism behind the modest grading multiples we measured in PSA 10 vs NM prices: with median gem rates near 89%, most One Piece chase cards are not condition-scarce, so the median PSA 10 sells for only about 1.5× the raw NM price. The exceptions worth grading come from the low-gem-rate sets, where the PSA 10 is genuinely harder to produce.

One caveat we apply to our own numbers: population only ever goes up, and the sets released most recently (OP-15, OP-16) will see their totals climb and their gem rates drift as submission volume normalises. Treat a very new set's gem rate as provisional until its population passes a few thousand.
